[[theatre:Alhambra Theatre & Dining]]’s [[production:2026 Morning After Grace]] is the kind of play that sneaks up on you. It opens with the recognizable mechanics of sitcom comedy — a disoriented morning after, missing memories, mistaken assumptions and mounting embarrassment — but before long it reveals itself to be something richer, sadder and far more profound. Written by [[person:Carey Crim]] and directed by [[person:Tod Booth]], this three-character dramedy finds humor in indignity, heartbreak in loneliness and, ultimately, grace in the stubborn human need to connect.

<!--more-->

Crim’s script is beautifully structured. One of its smartest early choices is having Abigail’s friend call at the top of the show, efficiently delivering exposition while also sharpening character and comic tension. It is a deceptively simple device, but an elegant one, orienting the audience without ever feeling forced. Even better is the brilliant use of the closet, which reveals crucial information through action rather than exposition. The production trusts us to watch, infer and feel the implications, and that trust pays off.

The setup is deliciously awkward: Abigail wakes up in Angus’s condo fully aware of what happened, but suddenly forced to sit with the embarrassment of it in daylight. Hickam plays those early moments beautifully, letting Abigail’s mortification mingle with the faint, rebellious thrill of having done something impulsive and a little reckless. One of the funniest beats comes when she finds women’s clothing and scrambles for an explanation, briefly deciding — or perhaps hoping — that Angus is a cross-dresser because the alternative, that she has slept with a married man, is far worse. It is sitcom-level comedy in the best sense, rooted in recognizable panic and social embarrassment. But the show never stays on the surface for long.

[[person:Kevin Anderson]] is terrific as Angus, delivering outstanding physical comedy with timing so exact it feels almost musical. Every awkward shift, delayed realization and flustered reaction lands cleanly, but Anderson never reduces Angus to a comic type. He gives him dignity beneath the confusion, allowing the audience to laugh at the situation without losing sight of the ache underneath it. [[person:Hillary Hickam]]’s Abigail is equally sharp, bringing wit, indignation and vulnerability in careful balance. She understands how to let Abigail’s defenses generate comedy while still revealing the loneliness and longing behind them.

Then there is [[person:Eugene Lindsey]] as Ollie, whose arrival transforms the play from an amusing morning-after tangle into something considerably deeper. Lindsey has a wonderful instinct for the play’s tonal pivots. The way Ollie does the math on how Angus and Abigail know each other is delicious, turning what could have been mere exposition into a scene of genuine dramatic electricity. Later, he delivers a spectacular monologue that silences the audience completely. It is an emotionally riveting moment, the kind that changes the air in the room.

One of the production’s greatest strengths is how evenly matched these performers are, both comedically and dramatically. No one is carrying dead weight, and no one is straining to dominate the stage. Instead, what develops between them feels organic and alive, with chemistry that ebbs, flows and deepens as the evening progresses. Their emotional connection grows in ways that feel earned rather than engineered, and that evolving bond gives the play its deepest resonance. The character development here is real, layered and persuasive.

Booth’s direction keeps the production agile enough to land the laugh lines while never losing sight of the emotional undercurrent. Several moments feel as though they could plausibly serve as the ending, yet each subsequent epilogue proves more satisfying and more beautiful than the last. That can be a dangerous structure in lesser hands, but Booth manages the rhythm carefully, allowing the play to unfold in emotional waves rather than hard stops.

Visually, the production is just as thoughtful. [[person:David Dionne]]’s set design is superb, detailed right down to outlets and light switches, creating a space that feels fully lived-in rather than merely suggested. There is something unmistakably television-sitcom adjacent about it — as if it were pulled straight out of *The Golden Girls* and rebuilt in the late 1990s or early 2000s — but that familiarity works in the production’s favor. It gives the audience an instantly legible domestic world, then lets the script complicate what appears comfortable and familiar. The kitchen peninsula is especially clever: angled at roughly 45 degrees, it adds dimension, improves sightlines and takes up less stage space without sacrificing realism. It is a smart, beautifully practical piece of scenic storytelling.

That lived-in realism extends all the way down to the props. [[person:Patti Eyler]]’s work as property master is especially effective in the production’s use of practical food. Fake food, while sometimes essential in theater craft, can sometimes kill a scene instantly, and this play depends on the rhythms of eating, handling and sharing food in ways that feel completely authentic. Here, the munchies are not just believable; they are essential to the show’s lived-in texture and comic reality.

What makes [[production:2026 Morning After Grace]] so affecting is the way it balances slice-of-life humanity with emotional depth. Some scenes play with the bright snap of a sitcom, while others hit with startling force, digging into grief, aging, regret and the terror of being known too well. The writing never treats those subjects with false solemnity, nor does it trivialize them for the sake of easy laughs. Instead, it lets comedy and pain coexist, as they so often do in life.

There is also something refreshing about seeing a relatively recent play produced locally in Jacksonville. So much of what gets staged here is already 20 or 30 years old by the time it reaches audiences. *Morning After Grace* feels contemporary not because it is chasing trends, but because its emotional language and comic rhythms still feel immediate. While its themes may land with particular force for middle-aged and older audiences, the comedy is accessible enough to reach nearly anyone, even if the subject matter makes it unsuitable for younger viewers.

With a fantastic script, an exceptional cast and direction that understands both the laughs and the bruises beneath them, Alhambra’s [[production:2026 Morning After Grace]] delivers an evening of theater that is hilarious, humane and unexpectedly moving. It sends the audience reeling from laughter one moment and absorbing a punch to the gut the next. By the end, what lingers is not simply the comedy of one strange morning, but the profound beauty of people finding connection after life has convinced them to expect less.
