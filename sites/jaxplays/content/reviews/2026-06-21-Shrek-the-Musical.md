An ogre gets kicked out by his parents, a princess waits far too long in a tower, a short-tempered ruler evicts a kingdom's worth of fairy-tale misfits and a donkey simply will not stop talking. That is the cheerful chaos of [[theatre:Alhambra Theatre and Dining|Alhambra Theatre & Dining]]'s [[production:2026 Shrek the Musical|*Shrek the Musical*]], a production that understands the best thing about this story is not that ogres have layers, but that everyone else does, too.

<!--more-->

If your relationship with *Shrek* begins and ends with the 2001 DreamWorks film, or even with a past production of the musical, Alhambra makes a strong case for seeing it again. The stage version keeps the movie's broad comic engine intact, but it expands the emotional map: Shrek's exile from childhood gets room to breathe, Fiona's years of waiting become more than a punchline, and Lord Farquaad's absurdity comes with a backstory that makes him funnier without making him softer.

That deepening is no accident. [[w:David Lindsay-Abaire]], the Pulitzer Prize-winning playwright who wrote the book and lyrics, elevates the familiar fairy-tale sendup by letting the jokes sit beside real loneliness, rejection and longing. [[w:Jeanine Tesori]]'s music keeps the show bopping from one comic set piece to the next, and it is easy to hear why Tesori and Lindsay-Abaire were nominated for the Tony Award for Best Original Score. *Shrek the Musical* may be one of the most underrated movie-to-stage musicals of the last 20 years. It deserves more credit than it gets for turning a pop-culture juggernaut into a stage story with its own heartbeat.

Alhambra's production starts building that world before the first line. Crickets and frog sounds ripple through the house, moss and camouflage-style canopy material hang overhead and green light washes not only the stage but the audience, pulling the room into Shrek's swamp before the story begins. It is a smart environmental gesture, and it pays off because the production keeps returning to that sense of place instead of treating the set as decoration.

At the center of it all, [[person:Alec Hadden]] is a delightful curmudgeon as Shrek. Hadden leans into the character's gruff defensiveness without flattening him into one long growl. His Shrek has a full spectrum of emotions under the scowl: at times furious, at times quietly openhearted, and always a fully realized person with hopes, dreams and frustrations. The route there moves through irritation, embarrassment, guarded hope and eventually the bruised tenderness that makes the story land. The performance gives the audience exactly what it wants from the ogre while making room for the man underneath.

[[person:Rachel Ann Kocher]]'s Fiona is bursting at the seams with energy and vitality. Kocher has the kind of comic timing that feels both precise and impulsive, and she uses it to keep Fiona from becoming merely quirky. Her Fiona is funny because she is desperate, disciplined, overprepared and slowly realizing that the fairy tale she has rehearsed in her head may not be the one she actually wants. The vocal and comic payoff is especially strong in "I Know It's Today," where Kocher, [[person:Kennedy Swartz]] as Young Fiona and [[person:Kayleigh Johnson]] as Teen Fiona become a breathtaking trio. You can hear [[person:Cathy Murphy Giddens]]' expert hand in the tight harmonies and clean musical shape of the number.

[[person:Jereme Raickett]] is a riot as Donkey, finding a lane that is both nostalgic and original. He knows the audience arrives with Eddie Murphy's voice somewhere in its memory, but he does not trap himself in imitation. Instead, Raickett plays Donkey as a fountain of need, chatter and unstoppable affection, building a performance with its own comic rhythm and emotional logic. His Donkey feels newly minted: quick, warm, wildly funny and alive to every shift in the room, turning a character everyone thinks they already know into one of the production's freshest pleasures.

[[person:Shain Stroff]] goes gloriously large as Lord Farquaad, which is no small feat for such a diminutive character. Stroff commands the entire stage from a lower altitude, never holding back while making sure every over-the-top choice feels intentional and rooted rather than merely played for laughs. The sight gag works, of course, and Stroff has the audience roaring, but the performance stands tall because his Farquaad is more than a walking punchline. He is a multifaceted villain, deeply rooted in pain and betrayal, with a short fuse, a long memory and a desperate need to make the world kneel before him.

The supporting work is full of scene-stealers. [[person:Ashia Collins]] is breathtaking as Dragon, bringing desire, depth, warmth and fury to a role that can become pure spectacle in less capable hands. She is also hilariously dismissive as Mama Ogre, a sharp reminder of how much comic authority she can pack into a smaller moment. [[person:Rodney Holmes]] is a physical and vocal comedy machine as Pinocchio, giving the character a bright, elastic specificity. Swartz is delightful across her multiple roles, especially as Young Fiona, and Johnson makes Teen Fiona pop without breaking the number's emotional line. [[person:Natalie Drake]] instantly captivates as the Sugarplum Fairy and Gingy, sending laughs through the room with a performance that knows exactly when to sharpen the edge.

The puppetry is surprisingly one of the production's most impressive achievements. What can become a stumbling block in other productions becomes a springboard here, giving the performers another way to build character, comedy and emotional life. Drake, [[person:Nick Cooper]], [[person:Jeremiah Burch]] and [[person:Mickey Titus]], handling Gingy, the Dragon head, and Dragon's stage right and stage left arms, do exceptional work conveying a full range of emotion through characters that could easily become technical chores. Cooper's lip-sync work on Dragon is particularly impressive, catching consonants right on the mark with Collins' vocal performance so that the puppet feels connected to the voice rather than merely accompanying it. With restricted range of motion and flexibility, these puppeteers still create rich, fully legible characters, turning technical limitations into some of the production's most expressive stagecraft.

Visually, the production's world keeps blooming outward. [[person:David Dionne]]'s set gives the performers room to move quickly through the story, while [[person:Chad Conley]]'s lighting ranges from whimsical and wild to grounded and place-setting. The programmable string lights built into the decorations in the house are especially effective, shifting from swamp fireflies to full-spectrum celebration during "Freak Flag." Costume and wig designers [[person:Camala Pitts]] and [[person:Dorinda Quiles]] do exceptional work throughout, with Dragon's look standing out as a particular triumph.

The sound design and audio execution are strong as well, beginning with that pre-show swamp texture and continuing through a production with a daunting number of featured roles. The microphones and backing tracks are well balanced, which is no small achievement in a show this busy. The audience can follow the jokes, the lyrics and the ensemble texture without feeling the technology fight the performers.

Director [[person:Jessie Booth]] keeps the show fast-paced and fun without rushing it. Her blocking uses Alhambra's distinctive layout to create moments of wonder while still keeping scene transitions crisp, and [[person:Tanner Lane]]'s choreography matches the material with playful, story-forward movement. The whole production has the bounce a family musical needs, but it also trusts the quieter emotional beats enough to let them register.

That balance is what makes this *Shrek* work. It is silly, green, loud and full of fart jokes, yes, but it is also a story about people who have been told they are too strange, too much or not enough deciding to stop apologizing for themselves. Alhambra's production leans into the fun without losing the ache underneath it. For longtime fans, it offers more world, more backstory and more heart than memory may have preserved. For anyone still underrating *Shrek the Musical*, this production makes a persuasive case to look again.
