[[theatre:Arts Collective Theatre]] could hardly have chosen a more fitting first fully staged production, or a stronger announcement of its new home, than [[production:2026 Victor/Victoria|*Victor/Victoria*]]. The musical is a glittering farce about performance, gender, survival and self-invention, and in this production it becomes something more: a joyful argument for the freedom to be fully, openly and theatrically yourself.

<!--more-->

Based on [[w:Blake Edwards]]' 1982 film, with music by [[w:Henry Mancini]], lyrics by [[w:Leslie Bricusse]] and additional music by [[w:Frank Wildhorn]], *Victor/Victoria* follows Victoria Grant, a struggling soprano in 1930s Paris, as she finds fame by posing as Count Victor Grazinski, a man performing as a woman. The premise is comic, but the heart of the story is not a gag. It is about what people risk when they hide, what they gain when they are seen and how performance can become a path toward truth.

That matters. In a moment when identity is too often treated as a political weapon, *Victor/Victoria* insists on the older, richer theatrical truth that people have always been larger than the categories built to contain them. The show understands gender as costume, mask, weapon, liberation and joke, sometimes all at once. Arts Collective Theatre leans into the glamour and the comedy, but it also lets the story's human stakes remain visible under the sequins.

[[person:Julia Blasi]] is beautifully assured as Victoria Grant and Victor. Her performance has the kind of confidence that never hardens into slickness; she remains strong, vulnerable and alive to the danger beneath the disguise. "If I Were a Man" becomes a defining early moment, not just because Blasi sings it with force, but because she lets the number reveal the ache inside Victoria's calculation. Her accent work is surprisingly precise, and her command of the role's emotional and musical range gives the production its center.

[[person:Josh Waller]] makes Carroll Todd a sparkling partner in crime. Waller's Toddy is witty, quick and generous, with comic timing sharp enough to keep the farce moving and enough warmth to make the friendship land. His chemistry with Blasi gives the show its essential engine: not merely a clever scheme, but a bond between two performers who understand that reinvention can be a matter of survival.

{{< figure src="/media/photos/2026-Victor-Victoria-03.webp" alt="Josh Waller as Carroll Todd gestures toward Julia Blasi as Victoria Grant in an apartment scene from Victor/Victoria." caption="Josh Waller's Carroll Todd becomes Victoria's co-conspirator and anchor, turning reinvention into both survival strategy and theatrical mischief." attr="Joshua Andrews / Jax Headshots" attr_link="https://www.jaxheadshots.co/" size="wide" >}}

The supporting performances are full of bright, specific choices. [[person:Joshua Bond]] is spectacular as King Marchan, the American nightclub owner whose suave certainty begins to buckle the closer he gets to Victor. Bond knocks "King's Dilemma" out of the park, finding both the comedy of King's confusion and the genuine feeling underneath it, and his "Almost a Love Song" duet with Victoria is devastatingly heartbreaking. [[person:Rashawnda Campbell]] is hilarious as Norma Cassidy and does not hold back from anything, which is exactly what the role needs. Together, Bond and Campbell turn King's and Norma's mismatched physical comedy into something outrageously funny, all sharp timing, wounded ego and escalating absurdity.

{{< figure src="/media/photos/2026-Victor-Victoria-02.webp" alt="Joshua Bond as King Marchan sings in a pinstripe suit against a blue digital scenic backdrop." caption="Joshua Bond's King Marchan lets suave certainty crack into confusion, comedy and genuine feeling as *Victor/Victoria* keeps rearranging the rules of attraction." attr="Joshua Andrews / Jax Headshots" attr_link="https://www.jaxheadshots.co/" size="wide" >}}

[[person:Gary Baker]] deserves real credit for making Henri Labisse such a laugh riot. His physical comedy is superb, but so is the character work underneath it; Baker knows how to make Labisse ridiculous without making him vague, landing each gesture and reaction with a wonderfully specific point of view. Waller has terrific chemistry with Baker, and their club-world comedy moves with wit and speed.

{{< figure src="/media/photos/2026-Victor-Victoria-06.webp" alt="Gary Baker as Henri Labisse sits with arms crossed at a nightclub table in Arts Collective Theatre's Victor/Victoria." caption="Gary Baker's Henri Labisse turns suspicion into comic fuel, landing each glare and gesture with the precise indignation that keeps the club-world farce spinning." attr="Joshua Andrews / Jax Headshots" attr_link="https://www.jaxheadshots.co/" >}}

[[person:Evan Gould]]'s Andre Cassell is the perfect emcee, drawing the audience into the club's orbit and making the room feel completely immersed in that glittering Parisian world. [[person:Alyssa Billings]] brings a lovely vocal shimmer to "Paris by Night," her vibrato giving the number a romantic lift. Across the ensemble, the production understands the period style without embalming it. "Trust Me" is especially fun, with [[person:Niki Stokes]]' choreography leaning into the time period while still feeling lively and theatrical.

{{< figure src="/media/photos/2026-Victor-Victoria-05.webp" alt="Alyssa Billings performs with a flower basket in a Paris street scene from Victor/Victoria." caption="Alyssa Billings brings a lovely vocal shimmer to \"Paris by Night,\" giving the number a romantic lift inside the production's glowing digital Paris." attr="Joshua Andrews / Jax Headshots" attr_link="https://www.jaxheadshots.co/" size="content" >}}

The only notable technical stumble comes in "Le Jazz Hot," where the energy dips and the microphones muddy the number's impact. In a production this polished, the issue stands out because so much else lands with such clarity. It does not derail the evening, but it briefly interrupts the otherwise confident musical momentum.

Visually, though, this production is spectacular. The highlight of the evening is the digital scenic design by [[person:Franklin Ritch]] and [[person:Britt McTammany]], whose projection backdrops are exceptionally brilliant. Club Chez Lui arrives in crisp, photorealistic detail; the Paris alley and apartment settings are equally sharp; and the "Paris by Night" world opens with elegance and depth. The projections do not sit behind the performers as flat decoration. They extend the room, deepen the scenes and make the stage feel almost cinematic. [[person:Joshua Andrews]]' gorgeous photography cannot capture the full effect of the projections, which are best experienced in the space itself. The images are not just beautiful; they are alive, and they help the production feel like a fully realized world.

That effect depends on the interplay between the projections and [[person:GoldenStage Productions]]' brilliant lighting design, and the collaboration is superb. Embedded light inside the scenic images gives the backdrops dimension, while the stage lighting pulls those images forward into the physical space. Haze, LEDs and movers help turn the venue into a nightclub world that glows, pulses and breathes. One audience member's delighted "How wonderful is this?" felt like the room saying the quiet part out loud.

[[person:Dirk Niedballa]]'s costumes add another layer of luxury and wit. The production looks expensive in the best sense: textured, tailored, finished. The suits, dresses and cabaret looks understand the show's gender play as both style and story. Even the smallest choices matter. Toddy's sock garters are a perfect touch, the kind of detail that tells you the costume design is listening closely to character.

Director [[person:Lee Hamby]] stages the evening with sweep and confidence, giving Arts Collective Theatre a production that feels far larger than a first fully staged effort has any right to feel. The scene transitions are fast, clean and often dazzling, with [[person:Susan Roche]]'s stage management helping the show move as if the next image is already waiting to bloom. The staging keeps the comedy crisp without losing the emotional thread, and it trusts the audience to follow both the farce and the ache.

That combination is what makes the production sing. *Victor/Victoria* is funny, glamorous and gorgeously theatrical, but its staying power comes from its belief that self-invention is not frivolous. Sometimes it is the bravest thing a person can do. For Arts Collective Theatre, opening a new home with this story feels almost like a mission statement: a theatre announcing itself through a musical about courage, community and the liberating thrill of stepping into the light.

As first fully staged productions go, this is not a cautious beginning. It is a declaration. Arts Collective Theatre has arrived with style, substance and a spectacular sense of what its new space can become.
