ShepardFest 2026 brings the restless, mythic work of Sam Shepard to St. Augustine for a concentrated weekend of theatre, film, lectures, scholarship and conversation at [[venue:The Waterworks]].<!--more-->

The festival invites audiences to experience Shepard as more than a playwright on a reading list. His career moved across theatre, film, poetry, music, acting and screenwriting, and his best-known works keep circling the subjects that make American stories uneasy: family, inheritance, reinvention, violence, longing and the myths people build around home.

Led by artistic director and co-founder [[person:Raines Carr]], ShepardFest is built for longtime Shepard devotees as well as people who may know him only from screen appearances in films such as *The Right Stuff* or *Steel Magnolias*. Carr says the festival "offers people that love theatre, love America and love stories about America, an entry point into some introspection about who we are."

[[venue:The Waterworks]], located at 184 San Marco Ave. in St. Augustine, gives the festival an intimate home for a concentrated cultural weekend. The city itself feels like a natural home for ShepardFest: shaped by history, memory and myth, with a walkable downtown where performances, conversations and community can spill into one another.

Carr, who grew up in Jacksonville and now lives in St. Augustine, says the location makes artistic sense even though the region is not one of the obvious Shepard landmarks.

"We're in America's oldest city," Carr says. "We're in a place steeped in Americana and history, and his work is all about: what is America?"

At the center of the stage programming is *[[production:2026 Buried Child|Buried Child]]*, Shepard's Pulitzer Prize-winning family drama, in a full production directed by [[person:Mary Claire Branton]]. The play begins with the shape of a domestic return and gradually exposes something stranger and more unsettling beneath the surface of an Illinois farmhouse.

ShepardFest also presents a staged reading of *[[production:2026 Kicking a Dead Horse|Kicking a Dead Horse]]*, a late-career Shepard work about Hobart Struther, a wealthy New York art dealer who heads west in search of authenticity and finds himself stranded beside a dead horse. The reading gives audiences a different doorway into Shepard's imagination: spare, isolated, comic and haunted by the American urge to start over.

Carr says the combination of lectures, films, a staged reading and full production is intentional: each event gives audiences a different decade, discipline or doorway into Shepard's work. "That's why we're doing lectures about him, showing his films, doing a stage reading and doing a full play, so you can get the gamut of different ideals in different time periods," Carr says.

The weekend expands beyond performance with lectures on historic set design, Shepard's place in American theatre and the costuming of Shepard characters. [Robert M. Dowling](https://www.simonandschuster.com/authors/Robert-M-Dowling/139896077), author of [*Coyote: The Dramatic Lives of Sam Shepard*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Coyote/Robert-M-Dowling/9781501195730), appears for a public book signing, adding a scholarly lens to the festival's theatrical and cinematic offerings.

Carr describes Dowling as "an authority on Shepard" and says having a scholar in the room expands the festival beyond performance alone.

Film is part of the conversation as well. Monday screenings of *[[w:Thunderheart]]* and *[[w:Days of Heaven]]* highlight Shepard's enduring presence on screen and connect the festival's live events to the landscapes, images and contradictions that run through his wider career.

Carr says that breadth matters because audiences often meet Shepard through only a small slice of his canon. "We only ever produce three of his plays and nobody ever does anything else," Carr says. "He's got tons and tons of work and books and short stories and all kinds of amazing things that just aren't shared."

For audiences new to Shepard, Carr says the work invites questions rather than handing down fixed answers.

"That's what a great artist does, right? They provoke," Carr says. "They don't answer, they provoke."

For visitors planning a fuller St. Augustine stay, ShepardFest lists nearby lodging options and walkable restaurants on its website, with "dinner and a show" packages expected in July. Tickets are available through ShepardFest and Light Works' ticketing page.

The long-term ambition is for ShepardFest to become an annual national festival in Northeast Florida. Carr says the 2026 edition already includes participants from multiple states, with hopes of drawing more national artists, Shepard collaborators and, someday, members of Shepard's family.

"I would like them to know that we're serious," Carr says. "It's a serious exploration of American art, and it can become a destination for that."

For Carr, the measure of the weekend is not just what happens in the room, but what audiences keep talking about afterward.

"My favorite movies I've ever seen, my favorite art I've ever seen, it's always a conversation afterwards," Carr says. "That you go eat, but you take the conversation with you."
