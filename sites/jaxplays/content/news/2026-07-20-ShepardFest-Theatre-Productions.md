ShepardFest will put Sam Shepard's work onstage at [[venue:The Waterworks]] this summer with a full production of *[[production:2026 Buried Child|Buried Child]]* and a staged reading of *[[production:2026 Kicking a Dead Horse|Kicking a Dead Horse]]* in St. Augustine.<!--more-->

The festival is led artistically by [[person:Raines Carr]], who co-founded ShepardFest with [[person:Andrea McCook]] in 2019. Carr said ShepardFest's return after a five-year gap is meant to establish the event as a recurring part of the region's cultural calendar. "We really want this to be a thing that happens every year, something you can count on," Carr said.

This year's festival's theatrical programming centers on *Buried Child*, Shepard's Pulitzer Prize-winning family drama about denial, inheritance and the violence buried beneath an American household's carefully maintained myths.

Carr called *Buried Child* Shepard's "largest, most famous" work and said it gives the revived festival a demanding first challenge for artists and audiences.

"Sam Shepard is not fooling around," Carr said. "This is rough stuff."

For Carr, the play's family reckoning still lands in the present tense.

"It's also couldn't be more topical to what's happening in our country with the American family, with the American person at the 250th anniversary of our country," Carr said. "The more I read it, the more I watch rehearsal, I'm just like, wow, I thought it wouldn't be topical, but this couldn't be more topical in terms of where we are trying to figure ourselves out."

[[person:Mary Claire Branton]] directs the ShepardFest production. The cast includes [[person:Britt Corry]] as Dodge, [[person:Andrea McCook]] as Halie, [[person:Tyler Hammond]] as Tilden, [[person:Josh Andrews]] as Bradley, [[person:Chad Boyd]] as Vince, [[person:Kaylee Byers]] as Shelly and [[person:Trevor Kluckman]] as Father Dewis.

Carr described the production's tone as "psychological dark comedy," with humor and disturbance deliberately pressed against each other.

"There is a lot of bizarre things that you're not sure you should laugh at, but they are meant to be funny," Carr said. "Shepard does this on purpose. He's trying to see where your line is between laughter, uncomfortability."

The production is also being built with The Waterworks in mind. Carr said the set, by stage designer Eddie Moncrief, is leaning into the restored St. Augustine venue's exposed brick, high ceilings and historic character rather than treating it as a neutral room.

"It's just such a unique historic space that we thought it would be interesting to put a sort of now historic drama -- it's 50 years old at this point -- into that space," Carr said.

ShepardFest will also present a staged reading of *Kicking a Dead Horse* at 2 p.m. Saturday, Aug. 1. The lean, late-career play follows a wealthy New York art dealer stranded in the desert with a dead horse, a shovel and the crumbling performance of the life he has built. In typical Shepard fashion, the Western image becomes a sharper reckoning with authenticity, masculinity, money and the American urge to reinvent the self.

Carr will perform as Hobart Struther, with Brooke Carr credited as co-actor and co-director. Because the reading cannot literally stage the play's desert action, including the physical business of burying a horse, Brooke Carr will handle stage directions while also serving as an "ethereal presence" connected to the wife Hobart has left behind.

"He has gotten everything, had everything, and is still searching for more," Carr said of Hobart. "Even though he had love, success, quote unquote happiness, it wasn't enough. There's always something more."

Carr said the reading will remain a staged reading, with scripts present, but will add selective theatrical elements including lighting, sound and simple staging.

"I don't like doing very busy stage readings," Carr said. "The actor and the words can be very compelling."

He said one reason for programming *Kicking a Dead Horse* is that many audiences are unlikely to have encountered it.

"I want to do premieres, things that people will have never seen and never been exposed to," Carr said.

Together, the two pieces offer a wide view of Shepard's theatrical landscape: the haunted family home of *Buried Child* and the stripped-down frontier myth of *Kicking a Dead Horse*. Both works return to questions that run through Shepard's writing: what Americans inherit, what they bury and what happens when the story they tell about themselves stops holding.
